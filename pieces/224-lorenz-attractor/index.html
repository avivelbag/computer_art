The Butterfly Effect — Lorenz Attractor in 3D

Two concentric spirals form each wing. The dashed red arrow marks the cross-wing jump — deterministic but practically unpredictable. Even knowing the equations exactly, you cannot say in advance which wing the next excursion will visit.

Press Nudge ε to spawn a second trajectory starting 0.001 units away and watch the two paths separate within seconds.

Who was Edward Lorenz?

Edward Lorenz (1917–2008) was an MIT meteorologist who in 1963 derived three coupled ODEs to model atmospheric convection. He discovered chaos accidentally: re-running a simulation from its midpoint produced a completely different forecast. The culprit was rounding — he had printed only three decimal places. He named the phenomenon the butterfly effect in a celebrated 1972 lecture: "Does the Flap of a Butterfly's Wings in Brazil Set Off a Tornado in Texas?"

What do the three variables represent?

The system models Rayleigh–Bénard convection — a thin horizontal fluid layer heated from below, the same mechanism that drives thunderstorms, ocean gyres, and the Earth's mantle:

x — angular velocity of the convection roll y — horizontal temperature contrast (warm vs cool side of roll) z — vertical temperature deviation from the linear background profile

The three parameters: σ (Prandtl number) is the ratio of kinematic viscosity to thermal diffusivity; ρ (Rayleigh number) is the normalised heating rate — chaos onset near ρ ≈ 24.7; β is a geometric aspect-ratio factor, classically 8/3 for a cell twice as wide as it is tall.

The equations (Runge-Kutta 4 integration, dt = 0.005)

dx/dt = σ(y − x) dy/dt = x(ρ − z) − y dz/dt = xy − βz

Both trajectories use fourth-order Runge-Kutta (RK4) — four derivative evaluations per step combined with 1:2:2:1 weights — giving fifth-order local error and keeping orbits faithful to the true attractor far longer than simple Euler integration.

What is chaos, mathematically?

A dynamical system is chaotic when it is deterministic, bounded, and exhibits sensitive dependence on initial conditions: nearby trajectories diverge exponentially. The Lorenz attractor's largest Lyapunov exponent is λ₁ ≈ 0.9 nats/time-unit, so a separation δ₀ grows as δ₀·e0.9t. Starting just 0.001 apart (one nudge), the two paths reach macroscopic separation in roughly t ≈ 8 time units — about 1 600 RK4 steps. The attractor is strange: it has fractal dimension ≈ 2.06 and no orbit ever repeats.
