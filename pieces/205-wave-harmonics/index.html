Fourier's Theorem (1822)

Any periodic function — no matter how complex its shape — can be expressed as a sum of simple sine waves. This insight underlies virtually every area of modern physics and engineering.

What Are Harmonics?

Harmonics are integer multiples of a fundamental frequency. If the base is 1 Hz, harmonics occur at 2 Hz, 3 Hz, 4 Hz, and so on. Each harmonic you activate refines the overall shape of the assembled wave — the upper panel shows each one individually; the lower panel shows their sum.

Gibbs Phenomenon

A perfect square wave has infinitely sharp corners. To reproduce them exactly you need infinitely many harmonics. With only 8 terms you can already see the Gibbs overshoot: the wave rings by ~9 % near each jump, and that overshoot never vanishes — it only becomes narrower as more harmonics are added.

Real-World Uses

  • Audio EQ: Equalizers boost or cut individual frequency bands to shape timbre — each band is one set of harmonics.
  • JPEG / MP3: Compression discards low-amplitude high-frequency harmonics that the ear or eye barely notices.
  • MRI: The scanner records Fourier coefficients in k-space; an inverse transform reconstructs the image.
  • Radio: Fourier analysis separates overlapping station signals by their carrier frequencies.
