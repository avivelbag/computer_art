Double-Slit Diffraction

Huygens-Fresnel Principle

Every point on a wavefront acts as a new source of secondary spherical wavelets. The total field anywhere downstream is the superposition of all these wavelets, each carrying the same frequency and phase as the original wave at that point.

A narrow slit doesn't merely let light through — it diffracts it. Each slit acts as a row of closely spaced point sources spreading energy in all directions. Even a single slit produces a broad diffracted beam whose intensity envelope follows sinc²(πa·sinθ/λ), where a is the slit width.

Path-Length Difference and Fringes

With two slits separated by d, waves travel different distances to any point on the screen. When the path-length difference equals a whole number of wavelengths, the two waves arrive in phase and reinforce each other — a bright fringe. When the difference equals a half-integer number of wavelengths, they cancel — a dark fringe.

Bright: d · sinθ = m · λ    m = 0, ±1, ±2, … Dark: d · sinθ = (m + ½) · λ

The fringe spacing on a screen at distance L is Δy = λL/d. Wider slit separation means closer fringes; longer wavelength means wider fringes. Use the sliders to verify this directly.

The Full Intensity Formula (N Slits)

For N identical slits of width a, separated center-to-center by d, the Fraunhofer diffraction intensity at angle θ is:

I(θ) = sinc²(β) × [sin(Nδ) / (N·sinδ)]² β = πa·sinθ/λ, δ = πd·sinθ/λ sinc(x) = sin(x)/x

The sinc² factor is the single-slit envelope — it suppresses or eliminates fringes where |sinθ| ≈ λ/a. The grating factor in brackets creates sharp principal maxima (at δ = mπ) and N−2 weaker secondary maxima between them. Young's Experiment and Quantum Mechanics

Thomas Young performed this experiment in 1801 using sunlight and two tiny holes in a card, measuring the wavelength of visible light for the first time. His result — that light is a wave — contradicted Newton's corpuscular theory and stood for nearly a century.

The quantum revelation: single particles (electrons, neutrons, C₅₀ fullerene molecules) fired one at a time through a double slit produce the identical interference pattern. Each particle passes through both slits simultaneously as a probability amplitude ψ. The Born rule |ψ|² gives exactly the intensity formula above.

The de Broglie wavelength λ = h/p connects a particle's momentum p to its wave behavior. An electron accelerated through 50 V has λ ≈ 0.17 nm — comparable to atomic spacings, which is why electron diffraction is used to map crystal structures.

Then reduce a to its minimum to widen the single-slit envelope and reveal more bright fringes. Finally, sweep λ through the full visible range and watch the fringe spacing scale linearly with wavelength.
