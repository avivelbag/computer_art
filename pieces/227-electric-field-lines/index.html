Charge Canvas — Electric Fields and Equipotentials

Coulomb's Law

The electric force between two point charges q₁ and q₂ separated by distance r obeys:

F = k·q₁·q₂ / r² (k ≈ 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²)

The electric field E at a point is the force per unit positive test charge: E = F/q. A single charge q produces a field pointing radially outward (positive) or inward (negative) with magnitude k|q|/r².

Superposition Principle

The total electric field from multiple charges is the vector sum of each individual contribution. This linearity allows any distribution to be decomposed into point charges:

E_total(x,y) = Σᵢ k·qᵢ·(r − rᵢ) / |r − rᵢ|³

This simulation evaluates the superposition at every step of the field-line trace and at every node of the potential grid.

Field Lines

A field line is a curve whose tangent at every point is parallel to the electric field at that point. Lines originate on positive charges and terminate on negative charges (or escape to infinity if there is no opposite charge nearby).

• Direction: the arrow tells you which way a positive test charge would accelerate.
• Density: where lines bunch together the field is stronger; wide spacing means weaker field.
• No crossing: the field has a unique direction at every point, so two lines can never cross.

The streamlines are traced by Euler integration: at each position compute the local E vector, normalise it, then step 2 px in that direction. The trace stops when it lands within 5 px of a negative charge, exits the canvas, or exceeds 2 000 steps.

Electric Potential and Equipotentials

The electric potential V is the work done per unit charge to bring a test charge from infinity:

V(x,y) = Σᵢ k·qᵢ / |r − rᵢ|

An equipotential is a curve on which V is constant — no work is done moving a charge along it. Equipotentials are always perpendicular to field lines because the electric field is E = −∇V, and the gradient is always perpendicular to the level sets of V.

The teal dashed contours are computed on an 80×80 grid using the marching-squares algorithm: each 2×2 cell gets a 4-bit code from its four corner values, and a lookup table emits the interpolated contour segment for that code. Ambiguous saddle-point cases (codes 5 and 10) are resolved by comparing the cell average to the iso-level.

Controls

• Click a charge — remove it.
