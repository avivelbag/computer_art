The Four Rules

  1. < 2 live neighbors → dies (underpopulation)
  2. 2 or 3 live neighbors → survives
  3. > 3 live neighbors → dies (overpopulation)
  4. Dead + exactly 3 → born (reproduction)

Game of Life — Turing-Complete Cellular Automaton

Conway's Biography

John Horton Conway (1937–2020) invented the Game of Life in 1970 while at Cambridge, publishing it through Martin Gardner's Scientific American column. Conway was searching for a simple two-dimensional rule system that could exhibit complex self-organizing behavior, inspired by questions about the theoretical possibility of machine self-reproduction.

Von Neumann's Self-Reproducing Automata

In the late 1940s, John von Neumann designed a 29-state cellular automaton capable of self-reproduction: a configuration that could read a description of any machine, including itself, and construct a copy. Conway's Life reduces this to just 2 states and 4 rules while retaining the same computational power — a remarkable compression of complexity into simplicity.

Turing-Completeness

Game of Life is Turing-complete: any computation describable algorithmically can be performed inside it. This was proven by constructing logic gates (AND, OR, NOT) from glider collisions, then wiring them into a universal computer. Paul Rendell built a Turing machine inside Life in 2000; since then, entire computers — including ones that run Life inside Life — have been constructed from gliders and still lifes.

The Gosper Gun and Unbounded Growth

Conway initially conjectured that no pattern could grow without bound. In 1970, Bill Gosper at MIT found the Gosper Glider Gun, which fires a new glider every 30 generations, disproving Conway's conjecture. This was the first "gun" — a period-30 oscillator that produces infinitely many gliders and was the key to proving Turing-completeness.

The Halting-Problem Connection

Because Life is Turing-complete, the halting problem applies: given an arbitrary Life pattern, there is no general algorithm to decide whether it will eventually stabilize, enter a periodic cycle, or grow forever. The fate of a pattern is undecidable in exactly the same sense that deciding whether an arbitrary program halts is undecidable.
